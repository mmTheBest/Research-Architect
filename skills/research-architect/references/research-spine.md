# Research Spine Framework

## Definition

The research spine is the smallest complete version of the paper’s argument.

Template:

```text
Problem: We study [specific object/process/task].
Motivation: This matters because [practical/theoretical/methodological reason].
Gap: Existing work does not adequately address [bounded gap].
Approach: We propose/use/test [approach].
Evidence: We evaluate it through [data/experiment/analysis/comparison].
Claim: We can support [bounded claim].
Scope: The claim holds under [conditions] and does not imply [forbidden overclaim].
```

## Good spine criteria

A good spine is:

- specific enough to design an experiment;
- narrow enough to support with available evidence;
- connected to literature;
- falsifiable or at least critically testable;
- useful for organizing figures and sections;
- honest about scope.

## Weak spine patterns

Avoid these:

- “This topic is important, so I will review it.”
- “We use a popular method on a dataset.”
- “The result is better” without defining the baseline and metric.
- “This explains a mechanism” without intervention, causal evidence, or careful limitation language.
- “Comprehensive” when the analysis is selective.

## Spine-to-draft mapping

- Title: object + approach + bounded claim.
- Abstract: compressed spine.
- Introduction: motivation + gap + contribution.
- Methods: approach and design details needed to evaluate the claim.
- Results: evidence chain in the order required by the claim.
- Discussion: interpretation, relation to prior work, scope, limitations.
- Conclusion: minimal restatement of the supported contribution.
