# Literature and Exemplar Mapping Framework

## Objective

Turn target papers and surrounding sources into a map of research logic, concepts, theories, methods, warrants, evidence standards, debates, and gaps.

## Source roles

| Role | Purpose | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| Primary exemplar | Supplies the overall research logic the user wants to learn from | Exemplar logic profile and adaptation plan |
| Secondary exemplar | Supplies one strong design, analysis, display, or rhetorical move | Adaptation plan and blueprint |
| Concept source | Defines the phenomenon, construct, task, or category | Introduction and conceptual framing |
| Theory source | Defines explanatory, interpretive, critical, or normative orientation | Theory, hypotheses, interpretation, or argument |
| Method source | Defines evidence-generation, analysis, or interpretive procedure | Methods or analytical framework |
| Comparison/warrant source | Supplies baseline, counterfactual, negative-case, rival-explanation, or counterargument logic | Design and validity |
| Benchmark/data/source-corpus paper | Establishes dataset, instrument, archive, protocol, corpus, or measurement norm | Methods, sources, or findings |
| Citation source | Supports an exact literature claim after verification | Draft citation support |

A single paper may have multiple roles. Each role is verified independently.

## Target-reference decomposition

For each target paper, extract:

1. **Research object:** phenomenon, population, case, corpus, process, relationship, concept, or task.
2. **Starting tension:** gap, contradiction, practical concern, theoretical puzzle, interpretive ambiguity, or normative conflict.
3. **Theory role:** definition, prediction, explanation, sensitizing concept, interpretation, critique, or emergent theory.
4. **Question-forming move:** how broad interest becomes an answerable question.
5. **Contribution type:** finding, explanation, interpretation, theory extension, method, dataset, framework, critique, or recommendation.
6. **Selection logic:** why these participants, cases, sites, periods, documents, sources, datasets, or experiments.
7. **Evidence construction:** how material is generated, gathered, screened, measured, or assembled.
8. **Analysis logic:** how material becomes findings, interpretations, explanations, or argument.
9. **Comparison or warrant:** baseline, control, counterfactual, negative case, rival explanation, counterargument, source criticism, or triangulation.
10. **Validity logic:** what makes the inference credible within the research tradition.
11. **Evidence sequence:** order in which the paper earns its central claim.
12. **Evidence-display sequence:** function of figures, tables, quotations, cases, process maps, models, and appendices.
13. **Rhetorical sequence:** function of sections and major argument moves.
14. **Limitation strategy:** how the paper bounds inference and contribution.
15. **Context-bound elements:** assumptions tied to the paper's specific field, population, setting, period, data, or access.
16. **Transferable strengths:** abstract principles the user's project can use.
17. **Copying boundary:** content that remains specific to the source.

Each strength should include a functional explanation:

```text
reference move
  -> why it works
  -> research function
  -> validity assumptions
  -> transferable principle
```

## Exemplar adaptation

Map every load-bearing move:

| Transfer ID | Reference Move | Why It Works | Transferable Principle | User-Project Analogue | Required Evidence or Material | Decision | Context Difference | Feasibility | Copying Boundary | Next Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Decision meanings:

- `TRANSFER`: the principle fits and receives a project-specific implementation;
- `ADAPT`: the function transfers while the implementation changes materially;
- `REPLACE`: another move is required to serve the same function;
- `OMIT_WITH_RATIONALE`: the function is unnecessary for the bounded project.

A strong adaptation explains mismatches. It does not reproduce the source's topic, sample, method, section order, or displays by default.

## Design-family inference

Infer a provisional design family from:

- the research question;
- theory role;
- evidence source;
- selection logic;
- analysis procedure;
- comparison or warrant;
- validity criteria;
- intended inference.

Use `design-family-grammars.md`. Mark hybrids and uncertainty explicitly.

## Gap and tension mapping

A proposed gap or tension advances when:

1. existing work leaves it unresolved;
2. the user can generate relevant evidence or argumentative warrants;
3. the resulting contribution matters to a reader;
4. the target-paper logic remains valid after adaptation or has a defensible replacement.
