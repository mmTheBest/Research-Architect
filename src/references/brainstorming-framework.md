# Research Brainstorming Framework

Use brainstorming to generate distinct research designs and questions. A polished title comes later.

## Candidate-generation lenses

### 1. Reference-logic lens

When a target paper exists:

- Which research moves make it persuasive?
- Which move creates the question?
- Which move creates credible evidence or argument?
- Which move controls alternatives and scope?
- Which principle transfers to the user's topic?

### 2. Adaptation lens

For each transferable principle:

- What is the user-project analogue?
- Which context-dependent assumption changes?
- Which evidence source or warrant must be replaced?
- What remains feasible under the user's constraints?
- What independent contribution becomes possible?

### 3. Problem or tension lens

What unresolved problem, contradiction, ambiguity, practical concern, theoretical tension, or interpretive dispute appears repeatedly?

### 4. Material lens

What can the user measure, observe, compare, trace, interpret, derive, synthesize, or argue from the materials and access they actually have?

### 5. Inquiry lens

Which logic of inquiry can answer the question:

- quantitative description or association;
- causal explanation;
- qualitative interpretation;
- case comparison, historical inquiry, or process tracing;
- mixed-method integration;
- theoretical, conceptual, critical, or normative argument;
- review or synthesis;
- computational, experimental, or design evaluation?

### 6. Warrant lens

What claim can be supported through the available empirical, interpretive, comparative, theoretical, or argumentative warrants?

### 7. Disconfirmation lens

Which negative case, rival explanation, sensitivity check, counterexample, contradictory source, or boundary condition would make the conclusion more credible?

### 8. Reader-value lens

What would a skeptical reader learn that prior work does not already establish?

## Candidate diversity

Two candidates count as different when they change at least one of:

- the research question;
- theory or conceptual orientation;
- unit, population, case, site, period, or corpus;
- evidence-generation strategy;
- analysis or interpretation logic;
- comparison, counterfactual, or warrant;
- contribution type;
- inference boundary.

Changing wording alone does not create a new candidate.

## Filtering rules

Advance candidates that are:

- answerable through a declared inquiry path;
- supported by feasible evidence, sources, or argumentative warrants;
- connected to literature;
- scoped enough for an auditable manuscript;
- clear about inference and transfer;
- compatible with or transparently adapted from the target paper.

Defer or reject candidates that are:

- dependent on unavailable resources without a credible substitute;
- broader than the evidence or argument can support;
- already resolved by prior work;
- driven by surface resemblance to the target paper;
- missing explicit premises, counterarguments, source-selection logic, or evaluation criteria for their design family;
- attractive mainly because the title sounds strong.
